Historical Overview

Jammu and Kashmir carries multiple layered meanings: the Union Territory since 2019; the former State (1947–2019); and the princely state (1846–1947). The broader Kashmir region also includes areas administered by Pakistan (Azad Jammu and Kashmir, Gilgit-Baltistan) and China, making the region's history intertwined with international geopolitics.

Ancient & Medieval Dynasties of Kashmir

Long before the creation of the princely state in 1846, Kashmir was ruled by several powerful Hindu, Buddhist, and Muslim dynasties that shaped the cultural and architectural landscape of the Himalayas:

Dynasty Reign Era Key Rulers & Significance
Karkota Dynasty c. 625 – 855 CE Lalitaditya Muktapida (reigned 724 – 760 CE): The most powerful empire-builder of ancient Kashmir. He expanded borders from Central Asia to Bengal and commissioned the magnificent Martand Sun Temple.
Durlabhavardhana: Dynasty founder; hosted the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang.
Utpala Dynasty c. 855 – 1003 CE Avantivarman (reigned 855 – 883 CE): Patron of arts and engineering; founded Avantipur city and oversaw river dredging works led by the engineer Suyya.
Queen Didda (reigned 980 – 1003 CE): A powerful female ruler who governed Kashmir with administrative skill and an iron fist.
Lohara Dynasty c. 1003 – 1320 CE Sangramaraja: Repelled the invasions of Mahmud of Ghazni.
Harsha (reigned 1089 – 1101 CE): Known as a highly controversial ruler who plundered local temples during financial crises. Hindu rule ended with Suhadeva's reign following Mongol raids.
Shah Mir Dynasty c. 1339 – 1561 CE Sultan Zain-ul-Abidin (reigned 1420 – 1470 CE): Popularly known as "Budshah" (The Great King). A highly progressive ruler who built canals, invited Persian artisans (introducing carpets, pashmina, and paper-maché), and rebuilt temples.
Sultan Shihab-ud-din: Great military strategist; expanded borders across northern India.
Chak Dynasty c. 1561 – 1589 CE Yousuf Shah Chak: The last independent ruler of Kashmir. Married to the legendary poetess Habba Khatoon. He was imprisoned by Mughal Emperor Akbar when the region was integrated into the Mughal Empire.

Detailed Chronological Profiles of Key Historical Rulers

👑 Raja Durlabhavardhana

Reign: c. 625 – 661 CE (Karkota Dynasty) | Territory: Kashmir Valley & Western Punjab

Founded the Karkota dynasty by breaking away from Hun dominance. He hosted the famous Chinese traveler Xuanzang, who documented a prosperous kingdom marked by peaceful coexistence between Buddhism and Hinduism.

👑 Lalitaditya Muktapida

Reign: c. 724 – 760 CE (Karkota Dynasty) | Territory: Central Punjab to Central Asia & Ladakh

Kashmir's most prominent military conqueror. He expanded the empire to Kannauj and Tibet, built his grand capital at Parihaspora, and commissioned the iconic Martand Sun Temple. He also repelled early Arab incursions in the northwest frontier.

👑 Avantivarman

Reign: c. 855 – 883 CE (Utpala Dynasty) | Territory: Kashmir Valley & Hill States

Focused on internal recovery and irrigation. He founded the city of Avantipur and built the Avantishwara temples. Under his patronage, the engineer Suyya dredged the Jhelum river to prevent floods and reclaim lands for rice cultivation.

👑 Queen Didda

Reign: c. 980 – 1003 CE (Utpala Dynasty) | Territory: Kashmir, Poonch, & Rajouri

One of the few sole female monarchs in ancient India. Despite a physical disability, she ruled first as queen consort, then as regent, and finally as monarch. She successfully navigated court conspiracies, maintained state borders, and consolidated administrative control.

👑 Sultan Zain-ul-Abidin ("Budshah")

Reign: c. 1420 – 1470 CE (Shah Mir Dynasty) | Territory: Kashmir, Jammu, Ladakh, & Baltistan

Celebrated as "Budshah" (The Great King) for his progressive policies. He established religious harmony, rebuilt damaged Hindu temples, and invited master artisans from Samarkand and Persia, introducing carpet-weaving, pashmina shawls, and papier-mâché to the local economy.

👑 Yousuf Shah Chak

Reign: c. 1579 – 1586 CE (Chak Dynasty) | Territory: Kashmir Valley

The last independent king of Kashmir. Married to the peasant-poetess Habba Khatoon, his reign was marked by internal clan conflicts. Summoned by Mughal Emperor Akbar to Delhi under false pretenses, he was imprisoned and exiled to Bihar, leading to Mughal annexation.

👑 Maharaja Gulab Singh

Reign: 1846 – 1857 CE (Dogra Dynasty) | Territory: Jammu, Kashmir, Ladakh, Gilgit, & Baltistan

Founder of the modern princely state of J&K. A general under Ranjit Singh, he consolidated the Jammu region and subsequently purchased the Kashmir Valley from the British under the 1846 Treaty of Amritsar, uniting diverse provinces under one crown.

👑 Maharaja Ranbir Singh

Reign: 1857 – 1885 CE (Dogra Dynasty) | Territory: Jammu & Kashmir Princely State

Succeeded Gulab Singh. He codified state laws under the Ranbir Penal Code (RPC), established telegraph and postal networks, and promoted education by funding schools and establishing archives for Sanskrit and Persian manuscripts.

👑 Maharaja Hari Singh

Reign: 1925 – 1947 CE (Dogra Dynasty) | Territory: Jammu & Kashmir Princely State

The last ruling Maharaja. Succeeded Pratap Singh and faced rising political demands led by Sheikh Abdullah in 1931. He enacted social reforms, including banning child marriage and opening temples to Dalits. In October 1947, faced with a tribal invasion, he signed the Instrument of Accession to India.

Formation of the Princely State

The Dogra dynasty of Dogra Rajputs from the Shivalik hills created Jammu and Kashmir through a series of treaties with the East India Company following the First Anglo-Sikh War. Key milestones:

  • 1820: The Sikh Empire recognised Jammu as a vassal state under Gulab Singh.
  • 1846 — Treaty of Amritsar: The British East India Company transferred Kashmir to Gulab Singh, recognising him as an independent Maharaja. Jammu and Kashmir became one of the largest princely states in India.
  • 1921: The state received a 21-gun salute for its Maharaja — the highest level of ceremonial recognition.

The Instrument of Accession

The Instrument of Accession signed by Maharaja Hari Singh in October 1947 transferred defence, external affairs, and communications to the Dominion of India — a legal compromise that became central to all subsequent constitutional arrangements for the region.

Modern Constitutional History

After accession, a distinct constitutional relationship evolved:

  • 1949–1954: Article 370 inserted into the Indian Constitution, granting special autonomous status.
  • 1957: The Constituent Assembly of J&K adopted its own Constitution (effective 26 Jan 1957).
  • 1965–1975: Significant political developments including elections and the Sheikh Abdullah era.
  • 1987–1990s: Insurgency and prolonged security challenges.
  • 2019 (5–6 August): Presidential Orders superseding the 1954 Order; Article 370 rendered inoperative.
  • 2019 (31 October): The Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act bifurcated the state into two Union Territories — J&K and Ladakh.
  • 2023 (11 December): Five-judge constitution bench of the Supreme Court unanimously upheld the constitutionality of the 2019 abrogation.
