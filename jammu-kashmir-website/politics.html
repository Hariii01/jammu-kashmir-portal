Politics & Governance

From princely rule under the Dogra Maharajas to the modern Union Territory administered under the Constitution of India — a factual overview of J&K's political evolution and current governance structure.

Political Overview

Jammu & Kashmir has experienced a complex political history shaped by princely rule, the events of 1947, and subsequent constitutional and administrative changes. This page provides a neutral, factual summary of key political developments and current governance structures.

Princely Era and Accession (pre-1947)

The princely state was ruled by the Dogra dynasty — Rajput rulers from the Shivalik hills. Maharaja Hari Singh was the last ruling monarch. Following a Pakistan-supported tribal invasion in 1947, he signed the Instrument of Accession to India in October 1947, which initiated legal and military processes involving India, Pakistan, and the local population.

Special Constitutional Status (1949–2019)

Article 370 of the Indian Constitution granted special autonomous status to the former state of J&K. This limited the central government's legislative powers for most subjects except defence, external affairs, and communications. The state had its own constitution, state flag, and permanent resident rules.

2019 Reorganisation

In August 2019, the Government of India abrogated Article 370 via Presidential Orders. The Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019 divided the state into two Union Territories on 31 October 2019:

  • Union Territory of Jammu & Kashmir — with a legislative assembly
  • Union Territory of Ladakh — without a legislative assembly

Current Governance Structure

As of 2026, Jammu & Kashmir functions under a dual-executive framework established by the Jammu and Kashmir Reorganisation Act, 2019 and the subsequent Transaction of Business Rules (amended in July 2024). Under this model, executive authority is split between the elected Chief Minister and the centrally appointed Lieutenant Governor.

Role / AuthorityScope of PowersAppointment / Selection
Lieutenant Governor (LG) Controls security forces, public order (police), land allocation, and senior administrative services (IAS/IPS transfers and postings). Appointed by the President of India on advice of the Union Cabinet (Home Ministry oversight).
Chief Minister (Omar Abdullah) Leads the elected Cabinet; manages state departments such as education, public works, tourism, agriculture, and forestry. Leader of the majority coalition (National Conference - Congress alliance in the 2024 polls).
Council of Ministers Advises the Chief Minister and oversees departments. Holds executive authority over state-list subjects, excluding public order, police, and land. Appointed by the LG on the recommendation of the Chief Minister.
Legislative Assembly Unicameral legislature of 90 seats (plus 5 nominated members and 24 vacant seats reserved for PoK). Can pass bills on State List and Concurrent List subjects (except police/order). Directly elected by voters; 5-year term. Last election was held in September–October 2024.
High Court of J&K and Ladakh Principal judicial body. Interprets laws and reviews the constitutionality of administrative actions. Judges appointed by the President of India in consultation with the Chief Justice of India.

Political Dynamics & Representation

Political life in J&K is characterized by the tension between local requests for statehood and the central government's security-first administration. The redrawing of borders post-delimitation has also reshaped regional dynamics.

The 2024 Legislative Assembly Elections

Held in three phases (September 18, September 25, and October 1, 2024), these were the first assembly elections in ten years and the first since the state's reorganization. The pre-poll alliance of the National Conference (NC) and the Indian National Congress (INC) secured a legislative majority, leading to the formation of the Omar Abdullah government.

The Transaction of Business Rules Conflict (2024–2026)

In July 2024, the Union Home Ministry amended the Transaction of Business Rules for J&K, significantly expanding the executive power of the Lieutenant Governor. The LG was granted sole authority over the posting and transfers of IAS/IPS officers, the operations of the Anti-Corruption Bureau (ACB), and the appointments of judicial officers, including the Advocate General. As a result, the Omar Abdullah administration faces frequent administrative friction as cabinet resolutions must navigate the LG's veto power, particularly concerning local police appointments, civic funding, and land use policy.
