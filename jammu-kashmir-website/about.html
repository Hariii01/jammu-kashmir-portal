Maharaja Hari Singh

The last ruling Maharaja of the princely state of Jammu and Kashmir (r. 1925–1949) — his reign, his decision in 1947, and the Instrument of Accession that shaped the region's modern history.

Biographical Overview

Loading biography extract…

Sir Hari Singh Bahadur (born September 1895; died 26 April 1961) was the last ruling Maharaja of the princely state of Jammu and Kashmir of the Dogra dynasty. His reign (1925–1949) coincided with the Indian independence movement and the pivotal events of 1947 that determined the region's future.

Early Life & Accession to the Gaddi

Hari Singh was the son of Amar Singh. In 1923, following the death of his uncle Maharaja Pratap Singh (who had no children), Hari Singh became the new Maharaja of Jammu and Kashmir at the age of approximately 28. He had previously served in the British Indian Army and was known for his interest in sports and modernization.

Early Controversies

  • 1931 — Agitation: Hari Singh faced a major agitation in Kashmir, led by Sheikh Mohammad Abdullah and others protesting Dogra rule and demanding political rights for Muslim subjects.
  • 1947 — Rebellions: Successful rebellions occurred in Poonch (against heavy taxation) and Gilgit-Baltistan (where the Gilgit Scouts mutinied) as independence approached.

The Instrument of Accession (1947)

After Indian Independence in 1947, Singh initially wanted Jammu and Kashmir to remain as an independent kingdom. He acceded to the Dominion of India to obtain the support of Indian troops against an invasion by tribal armed men backed by Pakistan.

Context of the Accession

As British paramountcy lapsed on 15 August 1947, Hari Singh was free to accede to India, Pakistan, or remain independent. He chose to negotiate a standstill agreement with both dominions while seeking independence. However:

  • Pakistan agreed to a standstill agreement; India did not.
  • In late October 1947, Pakistan-backed tribal militias (lashkars) invaded J&K from the northwest.
  • Facing an existential military threat, Hari Singh urgently requested Indian military assistance.
  • India's condition: J&K must accede to India first.
  • Hari Singh signed the Instrument of Accession on 26 October 1947 (received and accepted on 27 October 1947).

Later Life & Legacy

Hari Singh remained the titular Maharaja of the state until 1952, when the monarchy was formally abolished by the Government of India under pressure from Sheikh Mohammad Abdullah (who became Prime Minister of J&K). After losing his position, Hari Singh spent his final years in Bombay (Mumbai), where he died on 26 April 1961.

Historical Assessment

Hari Singh remains a controversial historical figure:

  • Defenders credit him with the decision to join India, which shaped the subcontinent's political geography.
  • Critics note his complicity in the 1947 Jammu massacres during the partition violence, his initial preference for independence over the welfare of his subjects, and the delayed nature of the accession decision.

His son, Karan Singh, became the constitutional head of state (Sadr-i-Riyasat) and later served as a Union Cabinet Minister and political figure in independent India.
